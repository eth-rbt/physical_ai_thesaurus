Physical AI often enters daily life first through disability and care. The lineage runs from the Lifeline emergency-call pendant (1974) and the Kurzweil Reading Machine (1976) — print-to-speech hailed as the biggest advance for blind readers since Braille — through Dragon NaturallySpeaking, which made voice a mainstream accessible input, to today's AI vision narrators (OrCam MyEye, Microsoft's Seeing AI) and proactive care companions (ElliQ). Each one raises the same question: does the technology extend agency and dignity, or shift new burdens of cost, dependency, and surveillance onto the people who rely on it?
