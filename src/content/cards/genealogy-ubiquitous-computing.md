Ubiquitous computing began as Mark Weiser's 1991 claim that the most profound technologies disappear into the environment, restated in his calm-technology essays and Amber Case's later design rules as a plea for machines that live at the periphery of attention. The lineage's most telling artifacts are deliberately dumb: an instrumented coffee cup (MediaCup), stick-on Smart-Its, a twitching length of cord that visualizes network traffic (Dangling String), a glass orb that glows one color (Ambient Orb) — nearly all of them ai:none or merely envisioned, because intelligence was supposed to live in the woven-together environment, not in any one model. Only at the commercial edge (Matter, Home Assistant) and the speculative frontier (Cordyceps) does a brain finally arrive, reframing ubicomp's distributed ecology as something that can now reason and act on its own.
