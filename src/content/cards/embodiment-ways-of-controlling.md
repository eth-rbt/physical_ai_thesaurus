Laid side by side, these projects sort less by what they do than by how much of you the steering demands: at the effortful, attention-heavy end you speak to an Echo or wave your whole body at a Kinect, while at the ambient end a Nest thermostat reads your presence and an IFTTT rule fires a trigger you wrote weeks ago and forgot. The richest devices refuse to pick one channel — an Apple Watch takes touch, crown, wrist gesture, and voice on a single wrist, a Ray-Ban pairs voice with a temple touchpad, a Humane Pin swaps the screen for speech plus gestures on a projected palm, and a Philips Hue bulb obeys switch, app, voice, routine, and motion sensor alike. The AI-embedded split is telling: the language-model devices (Echo, Ray-Ban, Humane) lean on conversational voice, but the most interesting control ideas come from research with no AI in them at all — Soli's radar micro-gestures, Skinput's taps on bare forearm, and Bricks' graspable handles show that "ways of controlling" is an interface question that long predates the intelligence now sitting behind it.
