These sort less by what they do than by how much of you the steering demands. At the effortful, attention-heavy end you dictate every word to Dragon NaturallySpeaking or throw your whole body at a Kinect; at the ambient end an IFTTT rule fires a trigger you set weeks ago and forgot, and Microsoft's EasyLiving watches who is present and reconfigures the room with no command at all. Between them runs a vocabulary of bodily input that barely touches a screen: Soli reads a micro-gesture in the air, a Wii remote turns an arm swing into the control, Skinput makes taps on bare forearm the input, Bricks asks you to grab handles, and a PaperPhone is steered by bending it. The intelligence is unevenly placed: Dragon's recognizer, the Humane Pin's language model, and Soli's and Kinect's learned classifiers do real interpretation, while the most physical techniques — the Wii, Bricks, PaperPhone, an IFTTT rule — carry no AI at all, a reminder that how we steer a thing is an interface question older than the models now sitting behind it.
