This lineage tracks a single move repeated across ninety years — placing a watcher inside the home and calling it care — and watching where care shades into surveillance. The precursors carry no AI at all (Zenith's 1937 Radio Nurse baby monitor, Marie Van Brittan Brown's 1966 motorized door-camera, the Lifeline fall button) yet already perform the core gesture of remote, always-on watching; the consumer line then absorbs intelligence in steps, from Dropcam's mere cloud streaming to Nest Cam's deep-learning person-and-package detection and Ring's neighborhood network and police partnerships, with Amazon Astro turning the fixed lens into a robot that patrols. EasyLiving's ceiling of tracking cameras and Cordyceps' speculative house of always-listening objects bracket the ambition and the dread of the whole genealogy: a home rendered fully legible to a machine that is, depending on who is watching, a nurse or a guard.
