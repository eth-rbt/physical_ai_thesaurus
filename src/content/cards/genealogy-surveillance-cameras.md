Read together, these projects perform one gesture for nearly ninety years — put a watcher in the home and call it care — and then show AI quietly changing what that watching means. The precursors run no AI at all (Zenith's 1938 Radio Nurse, Marie Van Brittan Brown's 1966 door camera, the Lifeline fall button) yet already do the whole job of remote, always-on watching. Nest Cam is the hinge: deep-learning person, package, and familiar-face detection turns raw video into named events, and Ring extends that into a neighborhood network with police footage requests, while Astro sets the lens walking. At the edges the same act is reframed by who holds it — Steve Mann's WearCam flips the gaze into sousveillance, and EasyLiving's ceiling cameras and Cordyceps' speculative always-watching house bracket the ambition and the dread of a home made fully legible to a machine that is, depending on the viewer, a nurse or a guard.
