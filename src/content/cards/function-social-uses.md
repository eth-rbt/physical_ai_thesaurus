What separates these is not technology but who, socially, the system is *for*. At one end sit ownerless common utilities — the Echo anyone in the room can wake, the Nest Hub the family glances at on the counter — and the Furhat in a lobby that turns to greet a stream of strangers; the Wii belongs here too, deliberately built for a whole household to take turns. At the other end are bonds tuned to one person: ElliQ for someone living alone, a Tamagotchi only its single keeper feeds and grieves. The sharpest cases are the in-between ones where the register is asymmetric or ambiguous — a Zenith Radio Nurse wiring one watcher to one watched, a PARO passed around a ward yet cradled one pair of hands at a time, and a Stochastic Parrot perched on one owner that nonetheless improvises "parrot-to-parrot" exchanges with other people's birds.
