The market is large and compounding: wearable healthcare devices were valued at **~$45.3 billion in 2025**, projected to reach ~$76 billion by 2030 (Source: MarketsandMarkets, 2025), and the FDA has now cleared **over 1,400 AI-enabled medical devices** — though ~76% are radiology, showing how thin the evidence base still is elsewhere (Source: The Imaging Wire / Innolitics, 2025–2026). What's newly possible is *intervention, not just tracking*: on-device inference now powers OTC continuous glucose monitors, wrist-worn AI arrhythmia detection, and wearable visual interpretation for blind users — AI that runs at the edge, fuses signals, and acts (Source: DelveInsight, 2025).

The whitespace is trust, reach, and equity. Continuous physiological and visual data raises acute privacy concerns, especially in eldercare where the monitored person isn't the buyer; false-alarm rates gate any device that triggers emergency or clinical decisions; and assistive and aging-in-place devices concentrate in North America at premium prices, leaving most of the world's disabled and elderly unserved.
